Simple ring-shaped spin-to-orbital angular momentum converter

Philip F. Chimento, Paul F. A. Alkemade, Gert W. ’t Hooft, Eric R. Eliel

Abstract

We demonstrate partial conversion of circularly polarized light into orbital angular momentum-carrying light where the circular polarization has the opposite handedness. This conversion is accomplished using the dichroic and birefringent properties of a subwavelength slit in a thin metal film [Chimento2011].

Introduction

The curious phenomenon of optical vortices arising out of nothing

Recently, we reported on how a subwavelength slit in a metal film can act as an optical retarder [Chimento2011]. A slit which is subwavelength in one direction and long in the other has two eigenpolarizations: transverse electric (TE) or parallel to the slit, and transverse magnetic (TM) or perpendicular to the slit. The attenuation constants of the TM and TE polarizations (T\TM, T\TE) and phase lag between the TM and TE components (Δφ) depend on the metal’s dielectric constant ε, the light’s wavelength λ, and the slit’s width w and depth d.

By careful tuning of these parameters, it is possible to construct a slit that has T\TE ≈ T\TM and Δφ ≈ π/2 rad; i.e., it behaves like a quarter-wave retarder whose fast axis is the TE axis [Chimento2011]. We take this one step further and make the slit circular, with a radius large enough that it still locally approximates a straight line on the scale of the light’s wavelength. In that case, the TM axis varies according to the position on the slit. It points radially outward and the slit thus acts as a space-variant quarter-wave plate with an azimuthally dependent orientation.

Experiment

To confirm this by experiment, we took a glass substrate of 0.5 mm thickness. On it we deposited a titanium adhesion layer of 10 nm thickness, and on that a gold film of 200 nm thickness. In addition to its adhesive properties, the titanium layer also damps surface plasmons, ensuring that they can only propagate on the gold-air interface.

We milled a circular slit, 5 µm in diameter and 200 nm thick, through the gold film using a focused ion beam. We also milled another circle halfway through the gold film, concentric with the first one but much larger, 20 µm in diameter, for the purpose of examining the surface plasmons launched from the ring.

We conducted the experiment using a narrowband continuous-wave titanium-sapphire laser (Coherent 899), with its wavelength tuned to 800 nm. We focused the beam from this laser weakly onto the glass side of the sample. The beam width at the waist was about 99999 mm, much larger than the nanostructure diameter of 5 µm, so that, effectively, the structure was illuminated with a flat wavefront. We used a microscope objective (NA = 0.55) to image the slit onto a CCD camera (Apogee Alta U1).

We measured both the polarization and the phase of the light transmitted through the structure as a function of position within the beam. To measure the phase, we placed the abovementioned structure in a Mach-Zehnder interferometer. To determine the polarization of the output state, we used a fixed linear polarizer and a computer-controlled rotating quarter-wave plate, from which we could extract the Stokes parameters by Fourier transform [Schaefer2007].
